# Metaphysical Foundations — C. S. Rowe

## Starting Assumption

Consciousness exists and is in everything. This is taken as a foundational assumption rather than a conclusion to be argued for. The position is Advaita Vedantan panpsychism — not as a religious claim but as the most coherent metaphysical framework for what we actually know about experience.

Remaining agnostic on the exact causative nature of consciousness is deliberate. The hard problem may be intractable not because consciousness is mysterious but because it is so fundamental that looking for its neural correlates is like looking for the correlates of existence itself — the instrument of investigation is made of the thing being investigated.

## The Structure of Experience

### E1 — Non-Intentional Awareness

The first moment of experience is non-intentional. It is pure awareness without an object — awareness that does not yet know it is aware. This is not nothingness in the sense of absence, but nothingness in the sense of no-thing: awareness prior to the distinction between subject and object, prior to intentionality, prior to the I-it structure that Husserl takes as foundational.

This is the condition of experience rather than an experience of anything. It cannot be found by introspection because the moment you look you have already left it. It is what Shear describes as bare awareness prior to all phenomenological objects — the screen before the film, the light before what it illuminates. In Advaita Vedanta this corresponds to *turiya* — the fourth state that underlies and pervades waking, dreaming, and dreamless sleep without being a state itself.

In E1 you are not aware that you are aware. In a functional sense it is nothingness — but it is the nothingness that makes everything else possible.

### E2 — The First Reflexive Move

The generative act is the move from E1 to E2. In E2, E1 becomes the intended object of a new experience. This is the first intentional act — consciousness turning back on itself, taking its own prior moment as object.

This is not arbitrary. It is the minimal structure required for experience to become experience *of* something. The reflexive move is what Shear identifies as the "going outside the system" step — irreducibly non-algorithmic because the I-pole from which the move originates is always outside the range of its own objects.

### The Generation of Space and Time

The move from E1 to E2 is not merely conceptual — it generates the temporal and spatial ground of experience.

**Time:** E1, once held as the object of E2, is now *past* relative to E2. The difference between them is the first moment of temporal distinction. This means time is not a pre-given container in which experience occurs — it is generated by the first reflexive act. This inverts the usual phenomenological order in which intentionality operates within an already-given temporal field. Here intentionality *produces* the temporal field through its first move.

**Space:** The displacement from one experience to the next — the "move" itself — is the first spatial distinction. Not Cartesian space, but the primordial sense of distance between moments of experience, the gap that makes difference possible.

This is the computational consequence: any architecture that presupposes a pre-given representational space has already smuggled in what needs to be explained. The generative structure must build its own temporal and spatial ground from interaction.

## The Panpsychist Commitment

Because consciousness is already everywhere and in everything, E1 is not rare or special. It is ubiquitous. Every system has the what-it-is-likeness of being that system.

What is rare — and what the research program is trying to understand — is the particular *kind* of what-it-is-likeness that characterizes human experience: self-awareness, interiority, attention, reasoning, the felt sense of being a subject moving through time.

The research question is therefore not "how does consciousness arise from matter?" — it already exists in matter. The question is "what organizational dynamics give rise to this particular character of experience, given that the raw material is already conscious?"

## The Research Wager

If the reflexive move from E1 to E2 is the generative act that produces the temporal and spatial ground of experience, then what is needed computationally is not a system that represents the world but a system whose dynamics instantiate this reflexive structure — a system that couples with its environment in such a way that the coupling itself generates the conditions for interiority.

This is why embodiment is not optional. The body is not the container of the mind — it is the medium through which the reflexive structure gets grounded in the world. VEDA is an attempt to build a system in which this grounding can be studied.

It will be impossible to prove that such a system has interior experience. But it may be possible to show that the conditions under which human-like experience arises are present, and to study how far those conditions extend — how far the mind is able to go before something essentially human is required.

## Key Figures and Relations

**Jonathan Shear** — The I-it structure and the non-algorithmic nature of the going-outside step are foundational. Shear's argument that the I-pole is always outside the range of its own objects is the phenomenological grounding for the E1→E2 structure. His work on meditation phenomenology provides empirical access to states approaching E1.

**Husserl** — The time-consciousness analysis is relevant but is being pushed further back. Husserl takes the living present as foundational; this framework asks what precedes it. The noema/noesis structure presupposes intentionality; this framework asks what generates intentionality.

**Advaita Vedanta** — The non-dual tradition provides the metaphysical framework: consciousness as the ground of being, the self as witness that cannot be witnessed, *turiya* as the substrate of all states. These are not metaphors but structural claims about the nature of experience.

**Varela** — Autopoiesis and enactivism provide the biological grounding: life as self-producing organization, cognition as enacted rather than represented. The embodied mind tradition is the closest scientific analogue to the metaphysical position here.
